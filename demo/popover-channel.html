The Mother Road

A 2,448-mile love letter to the American West

The image above captures the very essence of Route 66: a cowboy, stoic and weathered, holding a surfboard in front of a vintage gas station. It’s a jarring, beautiful contradiction—the rugged spirit of the dusty plains reaching out for the salt spray of the Pacific. For twenty-one days, I followed that same trajectory, tracing the asphalt veins of a country that seems to exist in two time periods at once.

The First Leg

Leaving the Skyscrapers Behind

My journey began under the shadow of the Willis Tower in Chicago. The first few days are a study in transition. You watch the steel and glass of the city dissolve into the brick-and-mortar reality of the Illinois heartland. By the time I hit Missouri, the air had changed. It felt thicker, smelling of damp limestone and diesel. I spent an afternoon wandering the Meramec Caverns—a sprawling underground labyrinth where Jesse James once hid—realizing that on this road, every stop has a ghost story attached to it.

In Oklahoma, the road opens up. This is where you find the Blue Whale of Catoosa, a giant turquoise monument to mid-century whimsy. It stands as a reminder that Route 66 wasn't just built for commerce; it was built for the sheer joy of seeing something you’d never seen before.

"The road is a way of being. It’s not just a path between two points, but a state of mind that rewards the slow and the curious."
Midway Through

The High Plains & Neon Sunsets

Texas is where the scale of the West truly hits you. Crossing the Panhandle, the horizon stretches so far you can practically see the curvature of the earth. I stopped at the Midpoint Café in Adrian—the mathematical center of the route—for a slice of "ugly crust" pie. From there, it was a straight shot into the neon glow of New Mexico.

Tucumcari is a town that refuses to let the 1950s die. At night, the Blue Swallow Motel flickers to life with a buzzing neon hum that feels like a heartbeat. I spent hours just watching the signs, realizing that while the interstate took the traffic away, it couldn't take the soul out of these places. The red dirt of the Painted Desert began to bleed into the landscape, signaling the start of the final, most brutal leg of the trip.

The Final Descent

The Mojave and the Pier

The last week was a gauntlet of heat and high desert. Arizona brought the Petrified Forest—ancient logs turned to multicolored stone—and a detour to the Grand Canyon that served as a humbling reminder of nature’s clock versus our own. Then came the Mojave. Driving through California’s desert in a car with questionable AC is a rite of passage. It is silent, shimmering, and unforgiving.

But then, the air changed. The scent of sagebrush was replaced by the bracing salt of the Pacific. Rolling onto the Santa Monica Pier on Day 21 felt like waking up from a long, vivid dream. I stood under the "End of the Trail" sign, looking at my car—caked in the dust of eight states—and realized I didn't want the drive to end. The cowboy had finally found his surfboard; I had finally found the ocean.
